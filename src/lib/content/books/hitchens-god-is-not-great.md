As a religious skeptic, I sometimes reach for literature that straddles the line between journalism and popular science. The author is a well-known critic of religion. Not as well-known as Richard Dawkins, but right behind him in terms of popularity. This book is his unconventional reflections on the nature and role of religion (various, not just Christianity) in society. As you can tell from the title and knowing the author, these are not flattering reflections on faith.

Hitchens's language is sharp and blunt. As in his debates, there's no squeamishness or beating around the bush. Hitchens cites numerous examples from history and his own life to demonstrate the absurdities of blind faith in certain beliefs, the practice of rituals, and the life decisions of people who base their judgment solely on holy books like the Bible or the Quran.

However, this book isn't as organized as, for example, Dawkins's "God's Deception." It's full of chaotic reflections loosely related to the current chapter. Sometimes I feel the author's judgments are harsh and contemptuous without justification.

An interesting, if chaotic, analysis of the errors of various religions. It encouraged me to further explore the subject of religious studies and the history of beliefs.
