This book was my first encounter with Japanese literature. I don't think I'd ever even read a book by an Asian author before.

As I read in the author's biography before reading the novel, the author was one of the pioneers of crime fiction in Japan. He was an admirer of Edgar A. Poe, which is reflected in his nickname – when read quickly, "Edogawa Ranpo" sounds like Edgar A. Poe (indeed!).

In my opinion, the novel has two parts. In the first, we learn the story of the main character (the narrator). His daily life, tragic love, and the beginnings of a murder investigation. I liked this part much more. It's dark and seedy, the action unfolds slowly, and with each page, more secrets and the dark side of the main character's situation emerge. However, as we get closer to the end of the novel, the action becomes faster, but it's presented in an inconsistent manner. The plot, however, takes a surprising turn, and the whole story ties together nicely by the end.

The plots are interrupted and quickly concluded. The hero's adventures naturally end very happily, but I'm deeply dissatisfied with the way the narrative unfolds at the end of the book.

A key strength is the portrayal of Japanese culture. The novel takes place in the 1920s, and for me, this period is a complete black hole in history. I have no idea what Japanese society was like during this period, and this novel provides a glimpse into the subject.
